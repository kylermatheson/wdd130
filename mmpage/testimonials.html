Statistics

According to a study by Dr. Gail Matthews at Dominican University, people are 42% more likely to achieve their goals just by writing them down. Harvard Business School found that graduates who had clear written goals earned ten times more than their classmates who didn't have such goals.

A study published in the British Journal of Health Psychology found that participants who kept a food journal lost twice as much weight as those who didn't track their food intake. Research from the University of Southern California suggests that habit tracking increases the likelihood of habit formation by up to 71%.

A study published in the European Journal of Social Psychology found that it takes an average of 66 days for a behavior to become automatic or habitual. According to research by Phillippa Lally and colleagues from University College London, forming a new habit relies on consistent repetition and can range from 18 to 254 days, depending on the individual and the behavior.
